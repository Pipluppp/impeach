## POINT OF INQUIRY OF SENATOR-JUDGE LACSON

Senator-Judge Lacson sought clarification on whether the Presiding Officer’s statement that conviction requires the affirmative vote of at least 16 Senator-Judges was a personal legal opinion or a ruling of the Impeachment Court. On the premise that it was a ruling of the Court, he further inquired whether, it has raised the level of discourse to a justiciable issue that only the Supreme Court has the legal authority to settle, should it be challenged by either of the parties.

The Presiding Officer confirmed that the statement was a ruling of the Chair. He noted that it had been clarified in response to an earlier inquiry by Senator-Judge Cayetano (A) and recorded in the approved Journal. As no objecton or appeal had been made, the ruling remained in effect. He added that whether the matter constituted a justiciable controversy was for the courts to detennine.

## MANIFESTATION OF SENATOR-JUDGE CAYETANO (A)

**Senator-Judge Cayetano (A) affirmed that he raised the question during the previous day’s proceedings.** He recalled that, upon receiving confirmation that the Presiding Officer’s statement constituted a ruling of the Impeachment Court, he did not pursue the threshold issue.

Referring then to Rule XXII of the Rules of Procedure on Impeachment Trials, Senator- Judge Cayetano (A) emphasized that orders and decisions of the Court may be acted upon without objection, or, if an objection is raised, shall be decided by a vote of the Members without debate, subject to the provisions of Rule VI on questions related to evidence. He likewise asserted that, although he had raised an objection during the previous day’s proceedings, he had not been afforded the period prescribed to explain his objections. Nevertheless, he acknowledged that, during the discussion on the point of order. Senate President Gatchalian has given everyone considerable latitude in explaining their respective positions and that he was instead allowed to submit his explanation in writing.

**Senator-Judge Cayetano (A) mentioned that the pending Cayetano v.** Aquino case before the Supreme Court likewise involved the interpretation of the phrase “all Members of the Senate.” He maintained that, under the High Court’s ruling, all incumbent senators remained Members of the Senate unless they died, resigned, or were removed from office with finality, and should therefore be counted in determining the two-thirds vote required for conviction. He added that he intended to prepare a memorandum for the guidance of his fellow Senator-Judges, particularly concerning the voting participation of Senator-Judges Marcoleta and Estrada. He acknowledged that, as of that day, the Impeachment Court had ruled that conviction on any Articles of Impeachment required the affirmative votes of at least 16 of the Senate’s 24 members.

## REQUEST OE SENATOR CAYETANO (A)

Senator-Judge Cayetano (A) requested time for the Impeachment Court to carefully study the legal issue of compelling witnesses to testify. He explained that while the Rules of Court generally prohibits forcing an accused person to take the witness stand, non-accused witnesses may be required to testify but can invoke the right against self-incrimination only in response to specific questions. He then proposed that if the prosecution intended to seek a subpoena, both the prosecution and the defense should submit memoranda in advance so the senator-judges can study the issue thoroughly before debating and ruling on it.

The Presiding Officer stated that the issue was premature beeause a formal motion has yet to be filed. He explained that the eourt would follow the same procedure it had used for earlier subpoena-related issues concerning the bank accounts of the spouses Duterte and Carpio and records from the Anti-Money Laundering Council (AMLC). Once the prosecution formally files its motion, he stated that the Court would require both parties to submit memoranda and would schedule oral arguments before deciding on the matter.
