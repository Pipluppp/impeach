The fi'amers could have required the Senate President to preside in all other impeachment trials. They did not. Neither did they prohibit the Senate from adopting another procedure. Kung malinaw ang Saligang Batas, hindi na natin dapat lagyan ng limitasyon na wala namang isinulat ang ating Konstitusyon.

As the Supreme Court reminded us in Civil Liberties Union v. Executive Secretary,1 “it is safer to construe the Constitution fi-om what appears upon its face.” The Court further held that the proper interpretation of the Constitution depends more on how it was understood by the people who ratified it than on the framers’ own understanding. Our duty, therefore, is to give effect to the constitutional text—not to read into it qualifications or restrictions that it does not contain.

This reading is reinforced by Article XI, Section 3(8), which expressly directs Congress to promulgate its Rules on Impeachment to effectively carry out the constitutional process. This provision is itself an express grant of constitutional authority. It recognizes that while the Constitution establishes the framework for impeachment, it entrusts Congress, in this instance, the Senate, with the responsibility of adopting the procedural rules necessary for the effective conduct of impeachment proceedings. Further, Article VI, Section 16(3) likewise recognizes that each House may determine the rules of its proceedings, a constitutional prerogative consistently respected by the Supreme Court as part of the Senate's institutional autonomy.

Rule LI, Section 136 of the Senate Rules was raised by our colleagues, which provides that amendments to the Rules by motion must be presented at least one day before their consideration. With due respect, that provision is inapplicable here.

The one-day notice requirement is textually and contextually limited to amendments introduced by motion. The amendment adopted by this Body was not proposed through a motion; it was embodied in Senate Resolution No. 430. As a formal legislative measure, a resolution follows a distinct procedural track and is not subsumed under the specific requirements governing motions imder Rule LI, Section 136.

By Senator-Judge Pangilinan Upon manifestation of the Presiding Officer, the following explanation of vote of Senator-Judge Pangilinan on the election of Senator Escudero as Presiding Officer of the Impeachment Court was deemed read into the Journal and Record of the Senate: The Constitution vests in the Senate the authority to elect a presiding officer in impeachment trials not involving the President.

I voted in favor of the election of a Presiding Officer because the Constitution does not require the Senate President to preside over an impeachment trial involving any impeachable officer other than the President of the Philippines.

Article XI, Section 3(6) of the 1987 Constitution provides: “The Senate shall have the sole power to try and decide all cases of impeachment. When sitting for that purpose, the Senators shall be on oath or affirmation. When the President of the Philippines is on trial, the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court shall preside, but shall not vote. No person shall be convicted without the concurrence of two- thirds of all the Members of the Senate.”

The text of the Constitution is clear. It expressly designates the Chief Justice to preside when the President of the Philippines is on trial. It does not mandate the Senate President to preside when the respondent is the Vice President or another impeachable officer.

At the same time. Article XI, Section 3(8) of the 1987 Constitution expressly provides: “The Congress shall promulgate its rules on impeachment to effectively carry out the purpose of this section.”Exercising this constitutional authority, the Senate, in plenary session, amended Rule II of the Rules of Procedure on Impeachment Trials to provide that, in impeachment cases not involving the President, the Senate President shall preside unless the Senate, by a majority vote of the Members present, elects another Senator as the Presiding Officer. I would like to add that this amendment was confirmed in the subsequent special session in plenary.

The amendment of Rule II likewise falls within the settled principle that parties acquire no vested right in procedural rules. In Tan v. Court of Appeals, G.R. No. 136368, the Supreme Court held that there are no vested rights in rules of procedure, which may be amended or repealed by the rule-making authority and generally apply to pending proceedings insofar as they regulate the mode of enforcing rights rather than the rights themselves. The amendment to Rule II did not create, diminish, or impair any substantive constitutional right. It merely prescribed the procedure by which the Senate, exercising its authority to promulgate rules for impeachment trials, may determine who shall preside in impeachment cases not involving the President.

The election of a Presiding Officer other than the Senate President in the impeachment trial of the Vice President was therefore not an exercise of a power inconsistent with the Constitution. Rather, it was the implementation of a provision adopted by the Senate pursuant to the rulemaking authority expressly granted by Article XI, Section 3(8) of the Constitution.

On the first issue, the Constitution provides that no person shall be convicted without the concurrence of two-thirds of all the Members of the Senate. With 24 Members of the Senate, a literal interpretation of this means that conviction requires the affirmative vote of at least 16 Senator- Judges.

This literal interpretation finds direct support in the 2000 case of Bayan v. Zamora, which concerned the Senate’s compliance with the two-thirds vote required to concur in a treaty. Despite having only 23 incumbent senators at that time, then-Sen. Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo was elected as Vice President. Therefore, there were only 23 senators.

The court held that the charter provides that the Senate shall be composed of 24 senators. Without a tinge of doubt, two-thirds of the figure, or not less than 16 members, is in unquestionable compliance with the requisite number of votes mentioned in Section 21 of Article VII.

It behooves, therefore, all of us to observe the same fidelity with the Constitution in this trial, which affects not only the mere ratification of a treaty but more so affects the substantial rights of the respondent as well as the sovereign will of the people as expressed in the overwhelming mandate given to her.

This is the prevailing law today, and whether we agree with it or not, we must abide by it. However, those who disagree are not without recourse, because this is without prejudice to the right of any party who holds a different view to seek judicial relief. And should the Supreme Court—which possesses the sole authority to interpret the Constitution by virtue of its power of judicial review—render a different ruling on this question other than what the Chair has stated, this court and this Representation shall faithfully abide by that ruling of the Supreme Court.

On the question of quantum of proof, given that impeachment is a sui generis constitutional proceeding, the standards of proof that govern administrative, civil, and criminal proceedings do not apply. However, this does not mean that senator-judges shall rely solely on their conscience in deciding this case. No less than the Supreme Court also said that “Decisions regarding whether to acquit or convict must be based upon clear charges supported by sufficiently clear and convincing evidence.”

Furthermore, let me be clear that the burden of proof in these proceedings rests upon the prosecution. It does not and will not shift. The respondent is afforded the presumption of innocence and has no obligation to disprove the charges against her.

Finally, we turn to the application of the Rules of Court in these proceedings. Rule I, Section 6 of the Rules of Court provides that the rules shall be liberally construed in order to promote their objective of securing a just, speedy, and inexpensive disposition of every action in proceeding.

We are also guided by the Supreme Court’s Bench Book for Trial Judges, which reflects a long-standing principle of adjudication. In case of honest doubt about the admissibility of evidence, it is a better policy to rule in favor of admission and let the senator-judges decide whether to give it little or no weight when deciding upon the case in the final outcome.

But let me be clear: a liberal construction of the Rules is not a license to ambush the opposing party, bypass due process, or introduce unverified evidence that would prejudice the fair and orderly conduct of this trial.

Accordingly, consistent with the demands of due process, fair play, and the orderly administration of justice, the Chair will construe the rules liberally so that, number one, the prosecutors may be able to adequately present their case; that the respondent may be able to rigorously test it; number three, that the senator-judges may be able to weigh the evidence for themselves; and fourth, so that the Filipino people may hear the whole and impartial truth regarding the allegations against the Vice President.

The Presiding Officer clarified that the scheduled date for deliberation only concerns the issuance of a subpoena duces tecum and ad testificandum for AMLC records, not the actual records. Should the subpoena be granted, he said that the documents would still have to be presented by the parties. He clarified that the impeachment court could not discuss AMLC records in Executive session as matters taken up therein could not be revealed to the public nor used in the proceedings. He further said that limiting the discussion of the evidence in executive session would effectively hogtie that piece of evidence. He said that the issues on whether the documents could be made public or could be legally obtained would be decided by the impeachment court after hearing the parties’ oral arguments.

## INQUIRY OF SENATOR-JUDGE VILLANUEVA

Senator-Judge Villanueva asked where the BIR box was currently being kept and when it would be returned to the Bureau of Internal Revenue. The Presiding Officer stated that the BIR box was in the office of the Senate Secretary, and that it would be returned in the same sealed condition to the BIR the following day.

## MANIFESTATION OF SENATOR-JUDGE HONTIVEROS

Expressing her support for the Presiding Officer’s ruling on the conduct of Executive sessions, Senator-Judge Hontiveros stated that Executive sessions are intended for Congress in the exercise of its legislative functions and are not contemplated under impeachment proceedings. Accordingly, she concurred with the Presiding Officer’s call for transparency governing the impeachment proceedings.
